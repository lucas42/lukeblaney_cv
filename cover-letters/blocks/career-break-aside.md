# Career-break aside

How (and whether) to mention the April–December 2025 career break in a letter. By default, this gets demoted to a brief aside near the end of paragraph 4, not as a paragraph of its own. Your current focus (see `current-focus.md`) is the headline.

## Default: one-line aside, embedded in the current-focus paragraph

Choose whichever fits the surrounding sentence:

- "…following a career break last year travelling around the world."
- "…after taking a year out last year to travel around the world before returning to London."
- "…having spent last year travelling round the world before returning to London full-time."

## When the break is itself a relevant feature (rare, only when the JD or company signals it)

For travel-related, transport-sector, or globally-distributed-team roles, the break can earn a sentence of its own:

> Last year I took a career break to travel the world: dozens of countries, a lot of public transport, and time spent with engineering and security people in several different markets. I'm now back in London full-time.

## When to skip it entirely

- The cover letter is already at the 350-word ceiling and something has to give.
- The JD or company is highly forward-looking and prior tenure is established elsewhere (CV).
- You're applying via a platform that already shows employment dates, so the gap is visible without needing narration.

## What never to do

- Don't lead with the break (a recurring weakness in older letters; see `user-cover-letter-patterns` memory).
- Don't use "time travelling". Accidentally ambiguous pun.
- Don't apologise for it or hedge ("I know this might look unusual…"). It was a deliberate decision; treat it as one.
- Don't pair it with the current-focus paragraph in a way that makes the break sound longer than the work since. By mid-2026 you've been back five months and the agentic-AI work is substantive.
